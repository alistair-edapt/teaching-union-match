Do I need to join a teaching union?

26 May 2026 · 8 min read

The short answer

  • No, joining a union is not a legal requirement in the UK.
  • But most UK teachers do join one — typically for protection if something goes wrong at work.
  • If you're not in a union and you face an allegation, capability concern or grievance, you handle it alone.
  • You usually can't join retrospectively — most unions have a 30-90 day waiting period for new members.
  • Membership is free for trainees and £1 in your first ECT year with most unions.

It's a question almost every new teacher asks at some point — usually after a colleague mentions which union they're in, or after the first time an allegation against a staff member at the school comes up. The honest answer is "no, but you'd be unusual not to."

Is it a legal requirement?

No. Trade union membership is a personal choice in the UK. Your employer can't make you join one and can't force you to leave one. You can work for your entire career without joining any union or alternative support body if you want to.

That said, the right to belong to a trade union is protected in UK law, and around three-quarters of teachers in England and Wales are members of one. The numbers in Scotland and Northern Ireland are similar.

So why do most teachers join one?

For most people, it comes down to one thing: support if something goes wrong at work. Teaching is a public-facing job involving children, and allegations — even ones that turn out to be unfounded — happen more often than you'd think. So do capability procedures, restructures, redundancies, grievances and HR disputes.

When that happens, a union can:

  • Give you an experienced rep to attend meetings with you (you have a legal right to be accompanied by a trade union representative).
  • Provide free legal advice on employment matters.
  • Represent you in a tribunal if it comes to that.
  • Help you think through whether to escalate, settle or move on.

You can pay for an employment lawyer instead, but their hourly rates start at £150–£300 and a full tribunal can cost five figures. Union subs are typically £150–£250 a year for full membership.

What you risk by not joining

The most common risk isn't strike action or politics — it's facing a difficult situation at work alone. A few honest things to know:

Allegations from pupils, parents or colleagues are not rare. Most are resolved quickly and most teachers never face a serious one. But when they do happen, an experienced and expert support on day one matters more than almost anything else.

Most unions have a waiting period. Typically 30 to 90 days during which they won't represent you on issues that arose before you joined. Joining only after something has happened is usually too late. You should check with your union before joining as this is not the case for everyone.

Your school's HR team isn't independent. They work for your employer. They may be perfectly fair, but their job is to protect the school, not you.

What unions actually do day-to-day

Beyond individual casework, most teaching unions also:

  • Negotiate collectively on pay and conditions (via STPCD in England/Wales or SNCT in Scotland — see the glossary).
  • Campaign on education policy — workload, curriculum changes, Ofsted, funding.
  • Train and accredit reps in schools.
  • Ballot for industrial action when negotiation fails. (This is the most visible thing they do, but for most members it's not why they join.)

How active each union is on each of those varies a lot. The methodology page explains the value framework we use to rate them.

What about the alternatives?

If you're not comfortable with the campaigning or industrial side of unions, there are options. NEU and NASUWT are politically active; Edapt is apolitical and focuses purely on individual employment support. Leadership-specific options like ASCL and NAHT exist for senior staff. So — should you join?

The honest answer is: almost certainly yes, in some form. Going completely unprotected isn't worth the saving. Which one you join is more interesting.

If you're a trainee or in your first ECT year, the decision is even easier — most unions are free during training and £1 in year one post-qualification. There's no good reason not to join something during that window.

FAQs

Can I join more than one union?

In theory yes, but very few people do. Most pick one trade union (or one alternative like Edapt) and stick with it. The exception is trainees, who sometimes join several while they're free to compare the offers.

Will my school see that I've joined a union?

Not unless you tell them. Your union won't disclose your membership to your employer, and most schools never know who's in which union (or any union at all) unless someone needs representation.

What if I disagree with a union's politics?

You can usually opt out of the political fund — the portion of your subs that goes to political campaigning. You stay a member, get all the protection, but don't contribute to political activity. If even that doesn't sit right, apolitical alternatives like Edapt exist.

How much does it cost?

For a full-time classroom teacher, £150–£250 a year is typical for most major unions. Cheaper for support staff, part-timers and ECTs. Read our detailed cost comparison.
