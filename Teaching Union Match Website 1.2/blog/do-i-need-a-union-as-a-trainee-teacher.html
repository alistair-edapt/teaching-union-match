Do I need to be in a union as a trainee teacher?

26 May 2026 · 6 min read

The short answer

  • If you're a trainee teacher in the UK, you should join a union or alternative — it's almost always free during training.
  • Trainees are the group most vulnerable to allegations (because of the placement structure) and least experienced in handling them.
  • Most unions offer free trainee membership for the duration of your course and a £1 ECT year 1 rate after.
  • You can try more than one while it's free, then narrow down at the end of training.

If there's a single piece of practical advice we'd give every PGCE, BEd, School Direct, Teach First and apprentice teacher in the UK, it's this: join a union or alternative support body before your first placement starts. It's free, it takes ten minutes, and you'll be glad you did.

Why training is the most vulnerable time

Trainee teachers face a few things that make union membership unusually valuable:

  • You're new to the profession. Safeguarding procedures, lone-pupil situations, and the boundaries of physical contact are things you'll learn on the job. Mistakes happen, even when you've done everything right.
  • Allegations can be made by pupils, parents or other staff. Most are unfounded or resolved quickly, but they happen. A trainee teacher who's been at a school for six weeks needs experienced advice on day one.
  • You're moving between schools. Multiple placements mean multiple sets of policies, multiple line managers, multiple unfamiliar environments — and no built-up trust to fall back on if something happens.
  • Your training provider's interests aren't always yours. Your provider wants you to pass; they're not necessarily your advocate if a placement reports a concern. A union rep is independent.
  • You don't yet have QTS. Allegations during training can affect whether you finish — which is why having a knowledgeable rep matters.

Trainee membership is usually free

Every major UK teaching union offers free or nearly-free membership during initial teacher training. Some sample rates as of 2025/26:

  • NEU — free for students and trainees (except some on the Assessment Only Route).
  • NASUWT — free during training, £1 in your first year post-qualification.
  • Edapt — free during training.
  • NAHT — has discounted aspiring-leader and trainee tiers.
  • EIS (Scotland) — reduced rate for probationers.
  • UCAC (Wales) — free for trainees and newly qualified.
  • INTO and UTU (Northern Ireland) — free for students/first-year NQTs.
  • SSTA (Scotland, secondary) — free for 16 months from August of your qualifying year.

Can I join more than one?

Yes, and many trainees do. While it's free, there's no good reason not to try a couple — particularly if you're undecided whether you want a traditional trade union or an apolitical alternative like Edapt. You'll see how their communications work, how easy they are to contact, and what their professional development offer looks like.

Around the end of your training year, pick one to continue with into your ECT period (where most unions charge just £1 in your first year).

What's the difference between options?

The main thing to think about is whether you want a trade union (negotiates collectively, can strike, often campaigns politically) or an alternative support service (apolitical, individual casework only). For a trainee specifically, the practical differences during training are small — what you'll mostly want is access to advice and representation if something goes wrong on placement. Both options offer that.

The political / collective-action side becomes more relevant once you're a paid employee with collective bargaining rights. We've written a longer guide to the decision for after you qualify.

Worth knowing about: the Early Career Framework

In England, once you qualify with QTS, you'll enter the two-year Early Career Framework (ECF) period. Your union (or alternative) will be relevant here too: if there are any issues with your induction, or if your school doesn't implement ECF entitlements properly, your rep can help you navigate that with your appropriate body. Scotland's probationary year (the TIS scheme) is similar.

FAQs

What if I'm an undergraduate teaching degree student?

Free trainee membership usually applies from the moment you start a placement that counts toward QTS. If you're in the early years of a BEd, you can still register early — most unions will set you up for free.

What about apprentices?

If you're on a teaching apprenticeship (postgraduate or undergraduate), you're treated as a trainee for most union purposes. You're also employed, which gives you employment rights from day one.

What if I'm doing Assessment Only Route (AOR)?

NEU charges AOR candidates, but most other unions still offer free trainee tier. Worth checking with the specific organisation.

What if I haven't joined and something happens on placement?

Talk to your training provider's pastoral / programme lead first — they have a duty of care to you. Most unions have waiting periods of 30–90 days for representation on issues that arose before membership, so joining now still helps for anything later.
